Why this exists

Alien land laws once barred Asian immigrants from owning property in over a dozen states — coded language, same intent. That pattern is repeating: since 2021, dozens of state legislatures have introduced bills restricting property, leasing, and research rights tied to national origin.

These bills move fast, in low-attention sessions, and rarely name a target group outright. By the time a community organizes, the vote has often already happened. This tracker exists to close that gap — one clear signal, before the vote, not after.

A history that repeats

The 2020s wave is not new — it's the third act.

1913

California's Alien Land Law bars "aliens ineligible for citizenship" — coded language aimed at Asian immigrants — from owning land.

1920s

Over a dozen states pass similar laws; the U.S. Supreme Court upholds them.

1943–1952

Alien land laws slowly repealed or struck down state by state, the last in the 1950s–60s.

2023–present

A new wave of state bills restrict land and property ownership by citizens of "countries of concern," again disproportionately affecting Chinese nationals and Chinese Americans.
